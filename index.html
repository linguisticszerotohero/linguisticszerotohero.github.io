1. Pragmatics: Maxim of Manner

The Maxim of Manner is a core component of Paul Grice’s Cooperative Principle. While other conversational maxims deal with what information is shared, the Maxim of Manner strictly governs how that information is presented to the listener, structurally and linguistically. To adhere to this maxim, a speaker must follow four distinct sub-maxims.

The Four Sub-Maxims of Manner:
  • Avoid obscurity of expression - Do not use overly complex terminology or jargon that the listener cannot understand.
  • Avoid ambiguity - Do not speak vaguely or leave room for multiple conflicting interpretations.
  • Be brief - Avoid unnecessary wordiness, rambling, and over-explaining.
  • Be orderly - Present information in a logical, chronological, or step-by-step sequence so the listener can easily follow along.
Significance & Reflection:

Understanding the Maxim of Manner is essential for effective communication for two main reasons:

i. Maximizes Efficiency: It prevents communication breakdowns by ensuring speakers structure their thoughts logically, saving the listener’s time and effort.

ii. Decodes Hidden Meanings: It helps us identify “implicatures”—hidden messages created when people intentionally break the rules to be indirect or evasive.

2. Phonetics: Consonants

Definition: A consonant is a speech sound that is produced with some restriction or closure in the vocal tract that impedes the air flow from the lung.

Consonants vs. Vowels: These are the three major differences between consonantds and vowels.

  • 1. Airflow Modification ("Roadblocks" vs. Free Flow)
    To pronounce a consonant sound, the air from you lung hits a "roadblocks" or a certain mouth structure like tongue, teeth, or lips to actively block, squeeze or redirect the air. Unlike how we pronounce vowels, your mouth stays wide open and the airflow is completely unobstructed.
  • 2. Production Mechanics (Physical Contact vs. Shaping Spaces)
    While Consonants rely on physical contact in the mouth to pronounce them, vowels rely only on shaping spcaes for it to be pronounced.
  • 3. Syllabic Infrastructure (The Language Backbone vs. Consonant Limits)
    Vowels are the backbone of language for every syllable must have a vowel. In contrary to consonants, you cannot build a proper syllable out of consonants alone.
